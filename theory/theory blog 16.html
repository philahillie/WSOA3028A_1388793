REFELCTION ON MY EXPERIENCE TAKING THE IMPLICIT BIAS TEST AND HOW IMPLICIT BIAS IMPACTS SOFTWARE DEVELOPMENT

Posted 04 July 2021 by Phila Hillie

The Implicit Bias Test was created by Harvard's Project Implicit several years ago. Its aim was to measure social attitudes and beliefs that people may or may not be willing to realise. The test has various categories such as a race test, a gender test and a test that interrogates potential biases against age groups that differ from our own. I took the age and weigh test to see what type of questions they would ask and was surprised to see how binary the approach to the questions were. I also found that although the test states that it is interrogating implicit biases (biases that are present but not consciously recognised), one cud argue that the way in which the questions were posed required you to be conscious or aware and "decide" what option you preferred. This makes me question whether it is accurate to describe the test as one that records your subconscious biases.

When looking further into the implications of implicit biases, I found an article that mentioned Amazon, their artificial intelligence matching (AI matching) software and how it was found that the technology held an unfair bias towards women. In 2018, Reuters deduced that Amazon's AI matching software had biases against women and that although Amazon claimed their recruiters did not rely on the rankings of the system, they admitted to considering them. The presence of biases in software and machines that are supposed to have no opinion is possible because these systems are developed and coded by humans who have implicit biases that influence the way they create and engage with the world. Additionally, a study showed that IT teams such as those of Google and Microsoft are 80% comprised of men and this subsequently influences the way in which software is biased against women.

When matching technology is created, rules around how it will work are developed by the developing teams. An example of this would be when a team wants to hire employees that can perform the way their top performing employees do and thus matching software that analyses the resumes of applicants and other information that employers find relevant and compare it to that of their existing employees. If potential employees are compared to existing ones in work environments that are male-dominated, one could argue that the initial set of rules that are developed will already be coded in favour of men and at a disservice to women because their resumes and other information that interests their male employers will not compare to that of a male applicant. It has also been said that weight is given to certain words.

One of the main reasons I started Milani Media House is because I was struggling to get a job. Initially, I thought it was only because I was a recent graduate and therefore a risky hire but I am starting to see that many more factors potentially play a role in why a women (and particularly a women of colour) will struggle to get hired in South Africa, a male and white dominated economy in which biases against race, gender and age ultimately play a role a significant role in your application process.

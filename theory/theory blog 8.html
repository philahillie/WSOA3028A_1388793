MAKING A FEMINIST INTERNET IN AFRICA - REFLECTION

20 May 2021

by Phila Hillie

I recently started reading about African Feminism within the digital space and was so happy to see that work on how we can reclaim the narratives of women, non-binary folk and the LGBTQIA community on the Internet is being investigated. Sheena Magenya wrote an article' "Making a Feminist Internet in Africa", that discusses the relationship Africans have with access to the internet, innovations made through the internet and how we are represented on he internet. She states that Africa is seen as a continent that needs technology and innovative ideas to solve existing problems such as famine, disease and drought instead of a continent where technology and innovation can be used for fun.

I resonate with this statement because, as an artist, I feel the pressure to create work that highlights existing issues that affect women as opposed to creating work that is centred around exploring artistic techniques and softwares that can create abstract works or products. There is an assumption that a female artist's work must highlight the violence and oppression that women endure and their persevering nature and I would love for women to be given the freedom to explore a range of different avenues of subject matter.

Magenya talks about how, although digital spaces have given women and the LGBTQIA community a platform to be seen and heard, they also mirror the violent and discriminatory nature of physical spaces. This statement reminds me of the #MenAreTrash movement where feminists were discussing how men are complicit in the violent behaviours of their peers because they do not speak out against perpetrators they know personally. It should also be noted that accessibility to the Internet is still a privilege and thus mirrors the unequal world we live in. Data prices in South Africa are alarmingly high and the rate at which the Internet consumes said data makes accessing the Internet and sharing content on the Internet extremely expensive.

There is power in being able to access stories and work that is reflective of your life and told through a voice that is similar to yours and that is why there is great value in championing feminism on the Internet. It is vital for African women, non-binary folk, the LGBTQIA community and people with disabilities to take up physical and digital space and initiatives such as the "Making a Feminist Internet' gathering and the "Feminist Principles of the Internet"are needed to make it possible.

I have attached a link to the "Feminist Principles of the Internet" to this blog post. The website states that they are a movement interested in creating safe spaces for African feminists to exist in and contribute to.
